International Tax Audit Defense

A tax authority in Germany or Switzerland has challenged the transfer pricing structure in your group. An adjustment is on the table, a second jurisdiction is involved, and relief in the other state will not arrive on its own. International tax audit defense in a Germany–Switzerland context means managing the position in both states from the outset — documentation, the closing conference (Schlussbesprechung), the objection, and mutual agreement preparation — under one coordinated line.

The legal framework

Three bodies of law govern international tax audit defense in a Germany–Switzerland context. Knowing which applies at each procedural stage — and how they interact — determines the defence strategy.

§ 1 AStG — German Foreign Tax Act

The German Foreign Tax Act codifies the arm's length principle for intercompany transactions. It empowers German tax authorities to adjust income from cross-border related-party transactions where the agreed price deviates from what independent parties would have agreed. § 1 AStG defines the documentation requirements, the consequence of insufficient documentation (reversal of the burden of proof) and the basis for estimated adjustments. It is the primary statutory instrument in every German TP audit.

Art. 9 DBA DE/CH

Article 9 of the Germany–Switzerland Double Taxation Agreement mirrors the OECD arm's length standard and, in paragraph 2, provides for a corresponding correction: where one state increases taxable profit on the basis of an arm's length adjustment, the other state shall make a corresponding reduction. This relief is not automatic — it requires a formal request, a substantiated position and procedural compliance within the applicable time limits. Art. 9 is also the gateway to the mutual agreement procedure under Art. 25 DBA DE/CH where the corresponding correction is refused or insufficient.

OECD TP Guidelines 2022

The 2022 OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines are the interpretive framework both German and Swiss tax authorities apply when assessing arm's length compliance. Chapter I establishes the arm's length principle and comparability analysis. Chapter VII governs intragroup services — the most frequently contested transaction type in German-Swiss structures — and sets the threshold between low-value-adding services (simplified pricing allowed) and high-value services (full comparability analysis required). Both German administrative practice (VWG Verrechnungspreise) and Swiss cantonal practice reference the Guidelines directly.

How audit defense works in practice

Effective audit defense is not reactive. By the time a formal assessment is issued, the options narrow. Each procedural step in the audit has a bilateral dimension that must be considered in advance — the position taken in Germany directly affects what can be claimed in Switzerland.

1 — Assess

Review the audit notification and existing documentation. Identify the specific adjustment basis, assess whether it is contestable and map the bilateral exposure — what the Swiss-side consequence would be if the adjustment stands, and whether a corresponding correction is available.

2 — Document

Assess the existing TP documentation against § 1 AStG and OECD Chapter I / VII standards. Where gaps exist, close them before the closing conference. Insufficient documentation shifts the burden of proof and eliminates the ability to contest an estimated adjustment.

3 — Closing conference

The Schlussbesprechung is the last opportunity to present the defence position directly to the tax authority before a formal assessment is issued. We prepare and attend the closing conference with a consistent position that anticipates the bilateral consequence in Switzerland.

4 — Objection and appeal

Where the assessment is issued despite contestation, a formal objection (Einspruch) preserves all procedural rights. Appeal to the tax court and, in appropriate cases, referral to the Bundesfinanzhof are available where the legal basis of the adjustment is arguable.

5 — Corresponding correction

Once a primary adjustment is final or sufficiently certain, the corresponding correction claim in Switzerland must be filed promptly under Art. 9 para. 2 DBA DE/CH. Time limits apply. The Swiss cantonal and federal authorities do not initiate this on their own — the claim and substantiation must come from the taxpayer's side.

6 — MAP preparation

Where the corresponding correction is refused or only partially granted, a mutual agreement procedure under Art. 25 DBA DE/CH is the route to elimination of remaining double taxation. MAP preparation requires a technically sound case file and coordinated presentation to both competent authorities simultaneously.

Why bilateral coordination from the start matters

A position that is defensible in Germany but inconsistent with the Swiss filing creates a problem at step 5. An adjustment that could have been avoided at step 3 becomes entrenched at step 4. The bilateral correction that should have been filed at step 5 is missed at step 6 because the time limit ran.

Each step is consequential for the next. Managing the defence across both states from one lead — rather than handing off between separate adviser teams — eliminates the inconsistency that coordination between two separate firms introduces. That is why this is designed as a single bilateral engagement.

Case example

German-Swiss group, management service fee dispute

A Swiss GmbH provides group management and administrative services to its German operating subsidiary. During a German tax audit covering three fiscal years, the fee charged is assessed as above arm's length and adjusted upward by EUR 2.4 million — increasing the German taxable profit for the audit period. The Swiss tax base is not reduced automatically.

The German subsidiary's existing TP documentation addresses the service categorisation under OECD Chapter VII but lacks a comparability analysis for the pricing methodology. The burden of proof has therefore shifted under § 1 AStG. The closing conference is scheduled for six weeks out.

Mandate: (1) Prepare supplementary documentation to address the comparability gap before the closing conference. (2) Present and defend the arm's length pricing at the closing conference. (3) File a corresponding correction claim with the Swiss cantonal authority immediately after the German assessment becomes final. (4) Prepare a MAP request under Art. 25 DBA DE/CH as a contingency where the Swiss correction is not fully granted.

Both the German objection and the Swiss corresponding correction claim are managed under one lead — with a consistent factual and legal position across both states from the outset.

What this illustrates

Documentation gaps are the most common reason a primary adjustment is difficult to contest. Closing conference preparation is therefore not optional — it is the last point at which the bilateral position can be aligned before the assessment is issued.

The corresponding correction claim and MAP preparation are not follow-on tasks. They run in parallel with the German defence from the moment the audit scope is known.

  • TP documentation review and gap analysis

Frequently asked questions

What triggers an international tax audit defense situation?

The most common trigger is a tax audit that challenges intercompany pricing between related group entities — goods, services, loans or IP. Other triggers include a profit allocation dispute involving a permanent establishment, an estimated assessment with cross-border effects, or a recharacterisation of an intercompany transaction. In each case the adjustment in one state creates a corresponding obligation in the other that does not enforce itself.

What is the difference between a primary adjustment and a corresponding correction?

A primary adjustment is the original correction by the auditing tax authority — for example, Germany increasing the German tax base by disallowing an intercompany price. A corresponding correction is the mirror adjustment in the other state: Switzerland reducing its tax base by the same amount to prevent double taxation. Under Art. 9 para. 2 DBA DE/CH, a corresponding correction is available but must be formally requested. It does not arise automatically.

How long does a mutual agreement procedure take between Germany and Switzerland?

MAP proceedings under Art. 25 DBA DE/CH typically take two to four years. Both states are BEPS Action 14 signatories with a commitment to resolve cases within 24 months on average. In practice, the quality of preparation at filing significantly affects duration — cases with a clear documented position and a well-substantiated file resolve materially faster than those filed reactively.

When should we involve an adviser in a transfer pricing audit?

Before the closing conference — ideally earlier. The Schlussbesprechung is the last opportunity to influence the audit finding before a formal assessment is issued. Once issued, the defence moves into objection and appeal. The bilateral dimension makes early involvement particularly important: the position taken in the German audit directly determines what corresponding correction can be claimed in Switzerland. Inconsistencies between the two positions are difficult to correct later.
